Part One

    Model Overview

    Reducing health costs is one way to improve individual life expectancy in the United States.

    From a few studies:

  • “The U.S. spends nearly 18 percent of GDP on healthcare, yet Americans die younger and are less healthy than residents of other high-income countries”

  • “In 2021, the U.S. spent nearly twice as much per capita on health as comparable countries: $12,197 compared to $6,514 on average.”

  • “Fifty-four percent of people with employer coverage who reported delaying or foregoing care due to high costs, reported that health problems of theirs or family members got worse.”

    • To identify potential cost savings of “pre-hospital telehealth programs”, I designed and implemented a Telehealth Cost Savings Model, using formulaic cost savings from the 2016 study: “Cost Benefit Analysis of telehealth in pre-hospital care”.

    The study, conducted in Houston, Texas, calculates patient and provider cost savings of the ETHAN telehealth system in redirecting eligible patients to less cost-intensive services; such as from ambulances to taxis and emergency departments (EDs) to urgent cares.

    Model results indicate immense 2024 state-level cost savings: ranging from 2.48% to 16.22%, as a percentage of annual health and hospital expenditure-alluding to immense potential savings from similar telehealth programs.

    Methodology

    A. Model Inputs

    To simulate the 2024 cost savings across thirty-one states in the U.S., the model utilizes the following inputs:

  • the study’s formulaic cost savings (from 2016) for patients and providers
  • annual health inflation rates from 2016 to 2021
  • 2021 regional price parities per state
  • total 2021 treat and release ED visits(1)
  • 2021 regional primary care related costs (PCRED) per region

    • The model calculates total patient and provider savings across select states in the U.S., combining these values to calculate total community savings.

    B. Patient Savings

    The model determined total patient savings, using 2021 PCRED costs and total ED visits per state in 2021. Missing Nevada ED visits data was estimated using dependent multi-series—or multivariate—forecasting in Python.

    Bi. Multiseries recursive forecasting is used to capture underlying trends impacting state ED visits. The eleven-year ED visits data is then split between "test" and "training" evaluation sets, as seen in Figure 2 below.

    The recursive method "LightGBM" is used to train the model. A lag of 1 ensures the model uses the most recent year's predicted values as inputs for the following year, ensuring the model is able to capture “short-term” trends; including changes in insurance provider policies, federal healthcare spending and health outbreaks (such as Covid19). While, “shorter” rolling window features ensure the model does not “smooth out” short-term, year-over-year fluctuations (Figure 3).

    C. Provider Savings

    Provider savings was calculated from the study's $103 per visit cost-savings. The model determines the predicted cost savings across thirty-one select states using the real price parity index (RPP), with the state of Texas as base. State ED visits are then used to determine 2021 state-level provider savings, which is then forecasted to 2024, using compounded annual healthcare inflation rates from 2022 to 2024.
